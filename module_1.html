LCYS_PCOM7E

Entitled "Launching into Cyber Security", this initial module covers topics from cyber security investment, secure coding and security architecture. Students are required to submit both short and long essays on these subjects, peer review submissions from other members of the cohort and write a python program to demonstrate the implementation of security controls identified as requirements in a medical scheduling application case study.

Module Learning Outcomes

Learning outcomes stated at the outset of the module, (listed below), were not specifically traceable to the assignment criteria as a one for one match, rather the intention was to interprete assignments and provide a point of view, which may require the e-portfolio reader to be somewhat familiar with computer science and cyber security matters.

  • Idenitify computer system components, consider and explain cyber security issues
  • Critically evaluate system vulnerabilities and mitigation approaches
  • Demonstrate an understanding of how object oriented programing can securly implement business system requirements

 

 

 

 

 

 

Collaborative Learning - Discussions One and Two

Both assignments took the form of each student creating a discussion board post on topic within the specified cyber security context. Students were given lattitude within the context,presumably to generate a variety of discussion topics while retaining a focused theme. All inital positions, discussion posts to cohort peers and summary posts defending or adjusting one's position would need to be supported by properly cited research.

Collaborative Discussion One

Discussion topic one, "Increasing cyber security investment in the energy industry", was peer reviewed by two class members, followed by my response below.
Review Post

The discussion topic, “Increasing cyber security investment in the energy industry”, was intended to highlight the need for ICS cyber security investment due to the potential for societal impact. Peer reviewer Steven Johnson contributed very qualified feedback, confirming the lack of military assistance, and identifying solutions offered by CISA.

The current CISA offerings are related to cyber security response preparedness, risk assessment and specialist assistance when incident response is required (CISA.gov, 2021). While such offerings will accelerate an organization’s ICS cyber security program, the government agency stops short of supporting solution implementation.

Response capabilities are the fourth of five high level functions defined in the NIST Cyber Security Framework (CSF), asset identification and protection being the first two (NIST, 2020). NIST CSF states functions do need to be applied in a particular order; that said, certain activity categories are effectively prerequisite to completing other functions. For example, identifying assets, communication flows and known vulnerabilities within an ICS system is required before an accurate risk assessment can be completed.

Although CISA provides free information and software (CISA.gov, 2021), the expertise required to collect and analyze the organization’s ICS environment is an expense borne by the ICS operator. An in-depth legal paper on cyber security investment incentives, cited by peer reviewer Shota Kameyama, noted only larger companies are likely to have dedicated cyber security staff due to cost (Kosseff, 2016). Reviewing tax credit research has identified long term job creation is not assured because tax credits often disappear with policy changes (Bowden, 2013). Retraining grants would allow ICS operators to retain employees with years of field experience and meet the demand for cyber skills without competing in the open market for talent (Maurer & Nelson, 2020) while simultaneously advancing competitiveness through increased digitization. Grant costs may be justifiable when weighed against the social impact of insecure critical infrastructure.

Collaborative Discussion Two

Discussion topic two, "Moving beyond firewalls", was peer reviewed by three class members, followed by my response below.
Review Post

The first sentence of the original post underscored the need to prioritize security technology investments that protect the user’s account and computing platform since many workers are not always protected by the corporate firewall. The pandemic driven requirement for remote working at scale proved not only feasibility, it also identified cost saving options (Gartner, 2020) and many workers actually prefer it (McCabe, 2020).

Authentication is often the first control that must be satisfied before granting access to any information technology system. Consequently I initially recommended, and still maintain, enhancing this protection mechanism reduces the risk of unauthorized access to an organization’s applications and data in a cost-effective manner. This claim is further supported by additional research I was able to locate separate studies by Microsoft and Google, both reporting greater than 99% reduction in account compromises once multi-factor authentication (MFA) is implemented (Maynes, 2019; Thomas & Moscicki, 2019). Post reviewer Nasim Akbary correctly identified MFA can be implemented through various technologies (Akbary, 2021), raising a question about two common MFA implementations over cellular phone which has both security and economic aspects for consideration.

Although mobile access is nearly ubiquitous across the globe, smartphone adoption still lags conventional mobile penetration (PEW Research Center, 2021) so organizational goals should dictate the MFA choice. One-time codes delivered to a user via short messaging service (SMS) are less convenient than a smartphone application but allow a wider number of users to safely authenticate. When an organization can be assured authorized users will have access to smartphone technology MFA notification applications are more convenient and provide a slight increase in prevention against targeted attacks (Thomas & Moscicki, 2019).

Multiple commercial software as a service (SaaS) options are now available to reduce cost and complexity of implementing MFA, placing this technology within the reach of small and medium organizations (Cisco, 2021). Conversely a top tier endpoint detection and response (EDR) product such as Crowdstrike can easily exceed several hundred thousand dollars for a larger organization (Cybersecurity Pricing, 2021), therefore it is not a solution every organization will be able to fund. The high cost of EDR was the impetus for recommending a reduction in network firewall investment as cost balancing measure. The original post did not clarify investment reduction should come through purchasing only firewall capabilities that are needed (Kelley, 2014), not a recommendation for removing firewalls altogether.

Reflections on Collaborative discussions

Both collaborative discussions appear to be exposing students to cyber security components and the ramifications of implementing those components, aligning with one of he module's learning objectives. In theory, civil discourse amongst peers serves to educate all parties since the response process required citations supporting any position rather than simply stating an opinion.

My professional cyber security roles have required both technical and written work that includes recommendations, but presenting suitable research related to the discussion and being willing to search for alternative points of view was a personal challenge. The first peer review process also demonstrated the value of peer discourse, fellow students did not hesitate to bring points forward for my consideration. In particular, the question regarding the effectiveness of tax credits forced me to not only research the topic specifically looking for measures of effectiveness but also consider alternative approaches to solve the workforce skills issue I still contend requires much more investment.

Unfortunately, I did not feel the same level of of engaged discussion with my cohort on the second collaborative discussion. The assignment had listed firewalls as a possible technology for consideration and the class had recently covered firewalls in a seminar on security controls and most students only discussed firewalls as a security control. Learning in a later module very few students had extensive cybersecurity experience, they may have been influenced to lead with that technology solution without any practical understanding of the limitations of this control mechanism. Since my position challenged the return on investment for modern enterprise firewalls, I recieved only one question on my position regarding multifactor authentication and there was no discourse regarding the limited effectiveness of firewalls. While network protection will always be a manadatory requirement, the inability to reliably inspect application layer attacks due to TLS encryption and the amazing levels of obfuscation present in modern fileless malware leaves me unconvinced that firewall investment should remain the priority. I now consider network based prevention table stakes, not a cornerstone of the defence strategy.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Vulnerability identification and mitigation

Since identifying security controls for the medical scheduling application was part of the case study assignment, module units included exposure to UML modeling and secure system design principles to assist skill development in this areas. Two formative artifacts were asked of the students, a wiki post on HTTP application threats and UML modeling digrams to illustrate threats.

The following summary was prepared for a seminar discussion related to vulnerabilites within http-based applications. Despite having performed numerous web application security tests professionally over the years I did take the time to locate multiple references so others in my cohort could do their own research if unfamiliar with web application testing and support my comments, (listed below), in the seminar.

  • The NIST Secure Software Development Framework (SSDF) defines the steps a software producer should take to ensure security is included in the development process and is abstracted enough that it can be applied to popular development models like agile and waterfall while simultaneously being suitable for software created for any industry (NIST, 2020). The NIST SSDF lists threat modeling as a requirement for an organization to produce well-secured software, stopping short of defining a specific approach, links to more detailed resources are included (NIST, 2020).
  • The Open Web Application Standard Project (OWASP) has been promoting secure code development for HTTP based applications for more than fifteen years. Similar to other threat modeling guidance it is incumbent on the organization creating the application to identify high impact, high likelihood events that warrant mitigation (OWASP, n.d.).
  • Table-top discussions can be used to validate the viability of certain types of threat scenarios (Veatch, et al., 1999) , which can ensure those that know the organization best can best assess the likelihood of a specific threat having an impact on the organization. Table-tops that are focused on organizational impacts can include members of the organization outside of the I.T. department such as legal, plant operations and finance. Scenario based threat model development is dependent on the input of domain experts, limiting the view of risks to purely technical elements can result in mitigations implemented that have little value and other risks being overlooked.
  • Attack trees or threat trees are another approach to identifying threats, while more technical in nature due to the requirement to deconstruct the adversarial goal into a series of steps the path variations can result in hundreds of scenarios for consideration (Widel, et al., 2019). Like table-tops, an attack tree based threat modeling activity will depend on individuals to identify likelihood and impact.

Reflection

These concepts are complex and would be revisited in later modules so only high level explanations were given in the seminars due to the limited time available. Students unfamiliar with cybersecurity could have found this portion of the module would not adequately support the analysis required to identify credible threats to the proposed application and mitigating controls. While the Essex module home identifies the potential need for additional programing study, students with limited exposure to cybersecurity may not identify, even through reading, the gaps in their knowledge that require further exploration to provide comprehensive analysis (Young& Ley, 2000). I found the exposure to UML as an illlustrative approach was informative but the attack defense tree mmodeling taught in a subesequent module provides a more measureable means of defending control decisions. Ironically my question about the use of attack trees was dismissed by the professor as seldom used yet it was a summative assignment in a later module.

Case Study Reflection

The

Writing the report took far more time than the nine hours indicated as recommended study hours and the 2000 word constraint really limited the level of description possilbe for a solution report. That said, the limitation does force the cyber security person to be very selective in what is communicated to the customer. This is not an unreasonable requirement, from my experience in the field lengthy reports are seldom read. The challenge I have observed in my professional practice is shorter reports with summary tables and high level descriptions lack the detailed analysis needed to define requirements for remediation of cyber security issues. This dilema was reflected in the professor's feedback regarding mitigation of insider threat concerns (see below).

Feedback Snippet

"...it is unclear how the elevation of privilege threat within the system will be addressed – ie how do we ensure a non-clinical staff is unable to view clinical report?"

Controls Identified In Report

Patient database security permissions are restricted to the dedicated account used by business logic application functions and the database commands needed for those functions (OWASP Foundation, 2021). Connection credentials used by an application entity must be instantiated at runtime, not stored in code (Engel, 2020). These measures minimize the consequences of an attacker gaining access to the business logic server, -- STRIDE threat category Elevation of Privilege --, then making a direct patient connection to retrieve all records.
The final trust boundary is STRIDE Information Disclosure threats from internal attackers. Administrative controls will be required to determine whether patient record access was authorized (Adler, 2019) since employees and consultants must view patient data through ASMIS. IT administrative access risks can be mitigated through products like transparent data encryption (TDE) (Oracle, 2021; Microsoft, 2021) to protect data at rest.

The insider threat was arguably had the lowest likelyhood of all threats identified in the report and corespondingly reflected in words allocated. While the database controls can provide an effective mitigation for both internal users and external clients since all parties use the same application, there was specific explanation of that concept due to word count limitations and the stated assumption that the report was directed to the organizations senior and I.T. management who possess a functional understanding of business management systems and issues. Highlighting the compensating administrative control due to the limited technical enforcement capability, seemed like a more direct linkage to insider threat concerns but since the controls were aligned to STRIDE catagories the messages were seperated by a couple paragraphs, potentially making the linkage less obvious. I anticipate I will need to continue to refine my communication skills through writing assignments in later modules and draw very clear lines to any items mentioned in a marking guide.

Although the programming assignment specified implementing one solution identifed in the report, I was encouraged,during office hours with my professor, to include a number of security controls in the program while remaining within the constraints of a single Python file. Consequently I used procedural approach encompasing a while loop and several functions to demonstrate the various roles that would be implemented in the application, preventative controls applied and reduce code complexity. Unfortunately, I was disappointed with the critique,specifically regarding the lack of python classes being used despite the fact that many in the Python development community consider classes an inappropriate solution in many cases (Cannon, 2017), and the assignment was a single Python file demonstrating a single function, not a fulsom application with user registration, password reset and so forth.
What I can take away from this is people have different mental models and there will be times in the security field that my customer's description or understanding of the requirements and mine will differ despite attempts on both sides to clarify understanding. I have recently been adding more books on management communications to my reading routine to help resolve these disconnects.
